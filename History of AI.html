History of AI

People have always entertained the idea of machines with their own intelligence and sentience. Just look at the countless books, movies, shows and other media about this topic, its benefits and its negative aspects.

One of the earlier mentions of AI was an idea similar to algorithmic text generation in Jonathan Swift's novel "Gulliver's Travels", where a machine made to generate ideas by turning handles to rotate wooden blocks with words on them was described.

Machines with intelligence are no longer a thing of the future. They haven't been since 1912, when Torres Quevedo built El Ajedrecista ' a chess-playing machine that could play endgames with two kings and a rook. El Ajedrecista always played the white king and rook, and could force checkmate in fifty moves or less.

Torres Quevedo arranged components like variable resistors, lead-acid accumulators, electromagnets and mercury tilt switches to control the machine's behavior. Electrical contacts under the chessboard detected the human's move, triggering relay circuits that activated the machine's mechanical arm to move its own pieces.

El Ajedrecista worked by breaking down chess positions into binary inputs ' piece present or not present ' a direct ancestor of how modern AI models process information.

Torres Quevedo didn't build it as a novelty. He wrote extensively about how its mechanical "mind" mirrored human reasoning. In many ways, El Ajedrecista is the great-grandfather of modern AI: its systematic counter-moves prefigured algorithmic decision-making, and its binary logic echoes the foundations of today's machine learning systems.
